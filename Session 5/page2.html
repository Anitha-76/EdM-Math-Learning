üéØ Learning Objectives ‚ñº

üìç The Scenario

"Three friends are arguing about who lives closest to school. Maya says she lives 3/4 of a mile away, Jordan lives 0.6 miles away, and Alex lives 4/5 of a mile away. Who lives closest? Who's farthest? How can we be SURE?"

Facilitation Questions:

  • "What makes this tricky? What tools or strategies might help?"
  • Record student ideas on the board
  • Emphasize: "Mathematicians are creative problem-solvers who choose the best tools for each situation!"

Three Strategies for Creative Problem Solving:

üéØ Strategy 1: The Benchmark Strategy

Using familiar reference points (0, 1/2, 1) to compare numbers

üìè Strategy 2: The Number Line Strategy

Visualizing where numbers sit in relation to each other

üîÑ Strategy 3: The Common Form Strategy

Converting all numbers to the same type (all decimals or all fractions)

üéÆ Interactive Strategy Demonstrations will appear here

Solving the Opening Problem:

Using Benchmark Strategy:

  • 3/4 is between 1/2 and 1, closer to 1
  • 0.6 is the same as 6/10, which is a little more than 1/2
  • 4/5 is also between 1/2 and 1, and it's actually close to 1

Verify by converting to decimals:

  • Maya: 3/4 = 0.75
  • Jordan: 0.6 = 0.6
  • Alex: 4/5 = 0.8

Answer: Jordan lives closest (0.6 mi), Maya is in the middle (0.75 mi), Alex lives farthest (0.8 mi)

üîë Key Point: "Notice how we used TWO strategies together? Creative problem-solvers often combine tools!"
üë• Interactive Activity 1: "Number Detective Teams" ‚ñº

Setup: Divide class into groups of 3-4

Set 1

2/3, 0.7, 3/4, 0.65, 1/2

Set 2

1.25, 5/4, 1.2, 6/5, 1 1/3

Set 3

0.4, 3/8, 2/5, 0.35, 7/20

Challenge:

  1. Order your numbers from least to greatest
  2. Place them on a number line
  3. Creative part: Each group member must use a DIFFERENT strategy to verify the order
  4. Prepare to defend why your strategy works

Roles Within Groups:

  • Strategy Scout: Uses benchmarks
  • Visual Mapper: Uses number lines
  • Converter: Changes all to same form
  • Presenter: Shares the group's thinking

‚ú® Interactive Activity 2: "Create Your Own Challenge" ‚ñº

Creative Application

Each student creates their own "real-world" comparison problem using mixed number types.

Requirements:

  • Must include at least one fraction, one decimal, and one whole number
  • Must tell a story (like the distance problem)
  • Must be solvable using benchmarks or number lines

Example Student Creation:

"At the bake sale, Marcus sold 2 1/2 dozen cookies, Aisha sold 2.75 dozen, and Chen sold 2 3/4 dozen. Who should win the prize for most cookies sold?"

Pair-Share:

  • Students swap problems with a partner
  • Solve using their choice of strategy
  • Discuss which strategy worked best and why
üí≠ Closing Discussion: "Math as Creative Problem-Solving" ‚ñº

Reflection Questions:

  1. "When you first saw mixed fractions and decimals, what felt challenging?"
  2. "Which strategy felt most natural to YOU? Why might someone else prefer a different strategy?"
  3. "How did having multiple strategies make you a better problem-solver?"
  4. "Where else in life do we need to compare things that look different but can be ordered?"
üåü Key Takeaway: "Today you discovered that being good at math isn't about memorizing one 'right' way. Creative mathematicians choose tools that make sense to them, combine strategies, and can explain their thinking. YOU are mathematical problem-solvers!"
üìö Differentiation & Extensions ‚ñº

For Students Who Need Support:

  • Provide pre-drawn number lines with some benchmarks marked
  • Start with simpler numbers (halves, fourths, tenths)
  • Use fraction-decimal equivalence cards as references
  • Partner with a peer mentor

For Students Ready for More Challenge:

  • Include negative numbers or numbers greater than 2
  • Ask them to create problems with very close numbers (like 0.66 vs. 2/3)
  • Challenge: "Can you find three numbers that are all within 0.1 of each other?"
  • Explore repeating decimals (like 1/3 = 0.333...)

Real-World Applications:

  • Comparing prices per ounce at grocery stores
  • Analyzing sports statistics (batting averages, completion percentages)
  • Comparing running/swimming times
  • Recipe conversions and measurements
